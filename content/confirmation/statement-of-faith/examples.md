**Past students' statements of faith.**

Reading a prior year's statements is the single fastest way to get a stuck confirmand unstuck. *"Oh — it's allowed to sound like that?"* is the reaction you want.

{{< callout type="important" >}}
**Examples are to prime the pump, not to template.** The whole point of this project is that it's *their* words. A student whose statement parrots an example has missed the point entirely — lovingly send them back to the drawing board.
{{< /callout >}}

## What a good statement tends to do

After reading a handful of past years, the common threads:

- **Starts specific.** A memory, a doubt, a person who matters. Not *"God has always been important to me."*
- **Names the hard parts.** The year that was dark, the prayer that felt unanswered, the doubt that hasn't quite gone away. Real faith has weather.
- **Lands in a specific commitment.** Not *"I will try to be a better person"* — something like *"I want to be the kid who sits with the new student at lunch,"* or *"I want to read a psalm before I look at my phone in the morning."*
- **Sounds like the student.** If you can't hear their voice in it, they haven't finished.
- **Names the church by name.** *"I want to be a member of [Church Name]"* is the point of the whole exercise.

## Published examples

{{< callout type="warning" >}}
**Holding space for examples.** We will publish 2–3 past student statements here as examples for future confirmands — but only with the student's (and parents') written permission, and only in anonymized form (first name or initials, no identifiable details about other people). **If you're the maintainer:** don't publish statements without going back to the students and parents; most will say yes, some won't, and that's fine.
{{< /callout >}}

## How to use examples in class

At the Session 12 workshop, or during the final parent class:

1. **Read one aloud** — the whole group listens.
2. **Ask:** what did you notice? What made it land?
3. **Hand out 2–3 examples on paper** and let students read them at their own pace.
4. **Ask each student** to underline one line they wish *they* had written — then ask them, what would be the *equivalent* line, in their own life?

That last step — the *equivalent line* — is where most first-draft statements get unstuck.
