**Part A of Session 11 · Prayer.**

**Focus:** Prayer focuses our attention on God and away from ourselves. When that happens, we open our hearts to God to become active in our lives, seeking community and justice for all humanity.

## Before class

- Lord's Prayer + scripture sheets printed for every student
- **Molding clay** — one piece per student
- A **cup or bowl of water** per student, plus paper towels
- Bibles

**Big question:** *Will you devote yourself to the prayers?*

## Opener — Heart Words

**Ask everyone to come up with a word or phrase that has "heart" in it.** Whoever comes up with one last loses — and has to face the consequences *(don't tell them what they are).* **No repeats.** If you'd like, write them on the board.

**Ask:**

- Why is the word *heart* so important in our language? *(The person who "lost" goes first.)*
- Why do people pray?
- Do you pray? Why or why not?

## Creed and Heart

**Ask:** Who remembers the Apostles' Creed?

- What is a **creed?**
- The word *creed* is connected to the words *"to give your heart."*

**Say:**

> We want to look at prayer as an **attitude of the heart.** Your challenge tonight will be to make a commitment to prayer.

## Read — Prayer in the Bible

*Ask volunteers to read:*

> ***Luke 18:1 — Then Jesus told his disciples a parable to show them that they should always pray and not lose heart.***

> ***Colossians 4:2 — Devote yourselves to prayer, being watchful and thankful.***

> ***1 Thessalonians 5:16–18 — Rejoice always, pray continually, give thanks in all circumstances; for this is God's will for you in Christ Jesus.***

**Ask:**

- What does each passage say about prayer?
- What makes that hard to do?
- How could it be possible to do what the scripture teaches? *(God will transform us to continually walk with and know him.)*
- What does it mean "not to lose heart" in Luke 18:1?
  - How might prayer keep us from losing heart?

## Molding Hearts Activity 💗

Give each student some clay and ask them to shape a **three-dimensional heart** (not flat).

While they're molding, teachers read aloud:

> ***Psalm 51:10, 17 — Create in me a pure heart, O God, and renew a steadfast spirit within me... My sacrifice, O God, is a broken spirit; a broken and contrite heart you, God, will not despise.***

> ***Romans 5:5 — And hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured out into our hearts through the Holy Spirit, who has been given to us.***

When finished, give each person a bowl and a cup of water. Ask them to set their clay hearts in the bowl and pour in a **small** amount of water.

**Ask:**

- What happens to the water?
- What would have to happen for the water to get *inside* the heart?

Pour more water on the heart.

**Ask:**

- Is there a connection between hearts and prayer?
- Why would God find broken hearts acceptable?
- How would prayer help lead us to broken hearts?

Give everyone a paper towel and ask them to pat their hearts dry, leaving them in their original shapes.

## Silent Prayer

Encourage students to sit comfortably with some space around them. Ask them to close their eyes and hold their clay hearts in their open palms.

**Say:**

> Imagine this is your heart. It's a little cracked and there may be small holes in it. As you hold it in your open palms, imagine offering it to God — not just once but over and over again. Minute after minute, you offer God your heart. Imagine the Holy Spirit flowing into the brokenness of your heart.
>
> How does it feel to have the Spirit in your heart?
>
> Say quietly to yourself: ***"Christ, be in my heart."*** *(Write the phrase on the board.)*
>
> Repeat the prayer. Allow other thoughts to flow away from you as you repeat, *"Christ, be in my heart."*

### Debrief

**Ask:**

- Was it hard or easy to keep repeating the phrase? Why?
- How did it feel to offer your heart to God?
- How did it feel to imagine the Holy Spirit flowing into and through your heart?
- If you prayed this prayer several times a day — *"Christ, be in my heart"* — how do you think it might or might not change you?

## Devote — Therefore

**Say:**

> **Heart** — the dwelling place for our emotions. The heart is our center.
>
> **Devotion** comes from the Latin *devovere,* meaning *to consecrate.* Devotion is a sacred commitment of the heart.
>
> When we devote ourselves to something, we respond with a *therefore* — an action.

Examples from the first three Acts 2:42 vows:

- **I will devote myself to the church's teaching, therefore I promise to...** *attend Sunday School, read and study the Bible, join a Bible-study group...*
- **I will devote myself to the fellowship, therefore I promise to...** *attend church, go on a youth retreat, attend youth group...*
- **I will devote myself to the breaking of bread, therefore I promise to...** *eat dinner with my family instead of in my room...*
- **I will devote myself to the prayers, therefore I promise to...** *(Write on the board.)*
  - *Pray before bed every night / say grace at meals even in a restaurant / pray for wisdom or strength when I'm having a problem at school or with friends / pray for others...*

Have each confirmand come up with their *own* "therefore" for devoting themselves to prayer.

## Closing Prayer

> *Merciful God, as a potter fashions a vessel from humble clay, you form us into a new creation. Shape us, day by day, through the cross of Christ your Son, until we pray as continually as we breathe and all our acts are prayer; through Jesus Christ and in the mystery of the Holy Spirit, we pray. Amen.*
>
> *— Book of Common Worship, p.108*
