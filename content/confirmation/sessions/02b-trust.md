## Opening

**Ask:** Someone refresh me on what I missed last week.

**Ask:** What is *trust?*

> *Firm belief in the reliability, truth, ability, or strength of someone or something.*

**Ask:** What are some actual practices or ways you can build your relationship with God? Ways to know God better?

*Possible answers:* read the Bible, pray, show love to others, worship, listen to Christian music, obey, serve, live with gratitude, seek like-minded people, live intentionally, live with optimism.

## Read — Hebrews 11

**Say:**

> Let's look at one small part of the Bible that lays down *why* to trust and *who did.*
>
> **Hebrews 11:1–38** is a list of the faithful who have trusted God "by faith." *(Faith and trust in God can be used interchangeably.)*
>
> Quick background: the book of Hebrews is a letter in the New Testament written to remind the people to remain faithful to Jesus. Two main goals: first, to show Jesus as superior — worthy of total trust and devotion; second, to remind the people to remain faithful to Jesus even through hard times.

*Ask volunteers to read:*

- **Noah** — Hebrews 11:7 *(cross-reference Genesis 6:13–22)*
- **Abraham** — Hebrews 11:8–9 *(Genesis 12:1–8)*
- **Sarah** — Hebrews 11:11 *(Genesis 21:1–7)*
- **Abraham again** — Hebrews 11:17–18 *(Genesis 22:1–18)*
- **Moses** — Hebrews 11:23–29 *(Exodus 14:21–30)*
- **Rahab** — Hebrews 11:31 *(Joshua 2:1–14)*

**Ask:**

1. What does Hebrews 11 tell you about God's people? About God in your life?
2. Based on what you've found in these stories, what do you think **faith** — or **by faith** — means?
3. Read aloud **Hebrews 11:1–3.** How does this help us fill out the definition of faith?

## Discussion — Do You Trust God?

**Ask:**

- Do you trust God? Why? Why not?
- Can you always depend on God?
- What about when sad things happen? Is God still being dependable then?
- Why is it important for confirmands to trust that God is gracious and merciful?

**Continuum exercise** *(after-outdoor variant):*

Have students stand in the middle of the room. Read each statement — students move to one side if they agree, the other if they disagree, or stay in the middle if they're unsure.

- God is always with you.
- God has a good plan for you.
- God always hears your prayers.
- When bad things happen, you trust it is always going to work out.

## Get Up and Move — Would You Rather?

**Stand in the middle of the room.** Ask each question; students move to a side of the room based on their answer.

1. Would you rather eat sushi ten days past its expiration date, or chew pre-chewed gum found on a New York City subway?
2. Would you rather drink a tablespoon of pond water, or a ten-year-old bottle of expired soda?
3. Would you rather eat sardines every day for a week, or always use the same fork, spoon, and knife used by every person in a local restaurant — without being washed?
4. Would you rather cross an ocean in an inflatable raft, or fly across the continent in an airplane that had been in a crash?
5. Would you rather have ten arms or ten legs?

*Now come back and sit down.*

**Ask:**

- Why would it be hard to trust eating, drinking, or traveling in these things?
- Why do these choices gross us out or make us nervous when we think about them?
- What are some reasons we wouldn't trust eating, drinking, or using these items?

**Share these concepts:**

- When we believe something will make us sick, or that something is unreliable to use, we cannot trust it. We want to avoid those items at all costs.
- Things in this world will fall apart or go bad, and we have a hard time trusting something that we think could cause us harm or put us in a difficult situation. *(And the last question — I was really just curious what you'd answer.)*
- **Only God is completely trustworthy.** He deserves our trust and confidence. And as we come to *know* him, we will come to *trust* him.
