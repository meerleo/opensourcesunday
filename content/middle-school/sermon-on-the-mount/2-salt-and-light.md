**Scripture:** Matthew 5:13–16
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

Jesus has just finished the Beatitudes. Without so much as a breath, he pivots from *"blessed are you…"* to *"you are the salt of the earth… you are the light of the world."* Notice what he didn't say. He didn't say *try* to be salt. He didn't say *if you get your act together, you might someday be light.* He said **you are.** It's an identity statement. The question isn't whether your students are salt and light; the question is what kind of salt and light they're being.

A few things worth knowing:

- **Salt, in the ancient world, was essential for life.** It preserved food before refrigeration. It flavored what would otherwise be bland. It was so valuable that Roman soldiers were sometimes paid in salt (hence *salary*). Salt that's lost its saltiness — contaminated, diluted — is useless. Jesus is saying: if a follower of mine loses the thing that makes them distinct, what's the point?
- **Light, in the ancient world, wasn't a flip of a switch.** An oil lamp was essential gear. A city on a hillside at night was visible for miles. You don't light a lamp just to hide it under a bowl — that's absurd. Light has a purpose: to be seen.
- **Verse 16 gives the reason:** *"let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven."* Not so you get noticed. Not so you look holy. So *God* gets noticed.

For middle schoolers, the quiet temptation is to keep faith *private* — something you do at church, not at the lunch table. Jesus is saying: that's not an option. You are already salt. You are already light. The only question is whether the world gets to taste it and see it.

## Center Stage
*What are the most important takeaways?*

{{< callout type="important" >}}
**You are salt and light. Your life is meant to make God visible and tasteable to the people around you — not so you get noticed, so God does.**
{{< /callout >}}

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- **A taste test:** two small bowls of the same snack (plain popcorn, unsalted tortilla chips, plain pretzels) — one unsalted, one salted. Keep them hidden at first.
- **A flashlight** + a cereal-bowl-sized container to cover it with
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers
- Index cards — one per student
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches (for opening)

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Invite a student to lead in prayer. Ask God that this group wouldn't be Sunday-only Christians — that we'd be the kind of salt and light that changes the flavor of our school this week.

### Catch Their Attention — The Taste Test + The Lamp

**Before the lesson:**
Set out two identical bowls. Fill one with the unsalted version of the snack, fill the other with the salted version. Keep the difference secret.

**During the lesson:**

**Part 1 — The taste test.** Pass out the unsalted snack first. Say nothing. Let them chew. Wait for the reactions.
> What are we tasting? Anything? Anyone want seconds?

Then pass out the salted version. Same food. Completely different experience.
> What changed? One ingredient. That's it.

**Part 2 — The lamp.** Turn off the overhead lights, or dim them. Hand a student a flashlight. Have them turn it on.
> What's this for?

Then take the flashlight and put it under the bowl.
> How useful is it now?

Say:
> Hold onto both of those. Tonight Jesus gives his followers two names — and they're the two things we just messed with.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 5:13–16

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> Last week we sat in the Beatitudes — Jesus cheering for the poor in spirit, the mourners, the meek. Without even stopping for breath, he pivots and says two of the most famous lines in the whole sermon. Listen for the pattern.

Read **Matthew 5:13–16** together. Go around the circle; each student reads 1–2 verses.

**Discuss:**

1. ***Jesus doesn't say "try to be" salt and light. He says "you ARE." What's different about that?*** — It's identity, not an assignment. You're already salt. You're already light. The question is how.
2. ***What does salt do?*** — Preserves. Flavors. Wakes up bland food. Wakes up bland *anything.*
3. ***What does light do?*** — Reveals. Guides. Warms. You can't hide from it.
4. ***Jesus says salt that's lost its saltiness is "no longer good for anything, except to be thrown out." What does he mean?*** — If a follower of Jesus loses the thing that makes them distinct, there's nothing there.
5. ***"A town built on a hill cannot be hidden." (v.14) What's that like today?*** — You can't miss a lit-up stadium at night. You can't miss a stage in a dark auditorium. Light gets noticed.
6. ***Look at verse 16. WHY do we let our light shine?*** — So people see our good deeds and give glory to **our Father**. Not to us. The whole point is to point people to God.

**Say:**
> Here's the thing Jesus refuses to let us do: keep our faith private. He doesn't say "be salty at church" or "light up for youth group." He says "you are the salt of the earth" and "you are the light of the world." Your whole week. Your whole school. Your whole table. Your whole team.
>
> And the point — *the whole point* — isn't to make you look like a good kid. It's to make God look like the good God he actually is. When your life tastes different and shines different, people get curious about who's behind it.

### Ponder the Possibilities

Discuss together:

1. ***Where in your life are you "hiding your light"? At school? At practice? Online? Around certain friends?***
2. ***What would "salty" friendship look like for you this week? Not preachy — actually flavored.***
3. ***Who in your life is genuinely salt and light? Someone whose life makes you want to know God more?***
4. ***Is there a place where your faith feels awkward or private? Why do you think that is?***
5. ***Jesus says we shine so others will *glorify God*. What's the difference between making yourself look good and making God look good?***

### Turn Toward Action — One Place This Week

Hand each student an index card. Say:
> Two questions. Write the answers on your card.
>
> **1. ONE place this week I can be salt and light on purpose.** (Your lunch table. Your team bus. That one group chat. One specific class.)
>
> **2. ONE specific thing I'll do there.** (Say hi to the kid who's alone. Thank a teacher on your way out. Refuse to join the roast. Invite someone to Crossroads.)

Keep the card in your pocket, bible, or phone case. Look at it on your way to school.

Close with prayer. Pray that this group wouldn't just come *to* church — that this group would *be* church, all week, in the places we already spend our time.
