**Scripture:** Matthew 7:7–12
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

Six verses. Three huge ideas. Jesus is bringing the Sermon on the Mount in for a landing, and the thread holding these verses together is one word: **Father.**

- **Verses 7–8 — Ask. Seek. Knock.** *"Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you."* All three verbs in the Greek are present tense and imperative — *keep asking, keep seeking, keep knocking.* Jesus isn't describing a one-time prayer formula. He's describing a posture of ongoing dependence.
- **Verses 9–11 — The Father Logic.** *"Which of you, if your son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Or if he asks for a fish, will give him a snake? If you, then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give good gifts to those who ask him!"* This is the heart of the passage. Jesus argues from the lesser to the greater. Even broken, imperfect human parents instinctively give their kids bread, not stones. *How much more* will your perfect heavenly Father give what you need? He's not saying we'll always get what we *want* — he's saying we'll get *good gifts,* because our Father knows what's good.
- **Verse 12 — The Golden Rule.** *"So in everything, do to others what you would have them do to you, for this sums up the Law and the Prophets."* The "so" is crucial. Because we have a Father who gives good gifts — because we're kids who have received generosity we didn't earn — we can afford to treat others with the same generosity. The Golden Rule isn't a standalone moral principle. It's the **overflow** of being a child of a generous Father.

**Two pastoral things to hold:**

1. Jesus is not teaching a vending-machine gospel. *"Ask and it will be given"* doesn't mean every prayer gets a yes. It means God hears, and God answers with good gifts — gifts shaped by his wisdom, not our wish list.
2. The "Father" language here is the same loaded word you flagged in Lesson 4. Don't skip past it. This entire passage rests on the premise that God is a *good* Father — and some of your students need to hear, *again,* that this is the Father they actually have.

## Center Stage
*What are the most important takeaways?*

**You have a good Father who loves to be asked. And because you've received so much, you can afford to treat other people the way you want to be treated.**

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- **A loaf of bread + a rock + a gummy fish + a rubber snake** (for the Father-logic visual). Bread + stone is the minimum; fish + snake doubles the impact.
- A closed door to knock on (literally) for the hook
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers
- Index cards — one per student
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches (for opening)

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Invite a student to lead in prayer. Tonight's passage is about a Father who loves being asked — so actually *ask* him to speak to this group tonight.

### Catch Their Attention — Knock Knock

**Before the lesson:**
Pick a door the group can see. If you have a co-leader, have them wait on the other side.

**During the lesson:**
Point at the door. Say:
> I want to try something. [Pick a student.] Stand up. Walk over to that door. Now — knock on it like you *really want* to get in.

Let them knock. The leader on the other side opens the door and says warmly: *"Come on in."*

> That was easy. Now imagine you spent **years** never knocking on that door. Never asking to be let in. Never even walking up. How weird would that be — if the person on the other side was ready to open it the whole time?

Say:
> Jesus is about to tell us something about a door. Your Father is on the other side of it. He's been waiting. You can knock.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 7:7–12

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> Six verses. Jesus is wrapping up the Sermon on the Mount, and he packs a TON into these six. Listen for one word he keeps coming back to: *Father.* Everything in this passage hinges on it.

Read **Matthew 7:7–12** together.

**Discuss:**

**Verses 7–8 — Ask, Seek, Knock:**
1. ***What three things does Jesus tell us to do?*** — Ask. Seek. Knock.
2. ***Fun fact: in the original Greek, all three of these are verbs that mean *keep doing it.* Keep asking. Keep seeking. Keep knocking. Why do you think Jesus frames prayer as ongoing?*** — Because prayer isn't a one-time form you submit. It's a relationship. You don't text your best friend once and call it a friendship.

**Verses 9–11 — The Father Logic:**
3. ***Jesus uses a ridiculous example. Read verse 9 again. What does he ask?*** — If your kid asks for bread, are you gonna hand them a rock?

Hold up the bread and the rock. Make it stupid and obvious.

4. ***Why does Jesus use such an absurd example?*** — Because no decent parent in the world would do that. The question is supposed to be embarrassingly easy.
5. ***Then Jesus says "IF YOU, THOUGH YOU ARE EVIL, know how to give good gifts…" What does that mean?*** — Even **broken** humans instinctively give their kids good stuff. How much more will a **perfect** Father do?
6. ***What's the "how much more" Jesus is arguing for?*** — If broken parents still give good gifts, God — the perfect Father — will **absolutely** give good gifts to those who ask.

{{< callout type="warning" >}}
**Pastoral note — the "Father" word again.** Like in Lesson 4, don't rush past this. If some students hear "Father" and flinch, name it out loud:
> Some of us have dads who are amazing. Some of us have dads who aren't. Jesus is not saying *God is like your dad.* He's saying *God is the Father every one of you was made to have.* The kind who gives bread, not stones. Every time.
{{< /callout >}}

7. ***Does "ask and it will be given" mean I always get what I want?*** — No. It means God gives **good gifts.** Sometimes what you asked for wasn't actually good. God's answers are shaped by his wisdom, not your wish list. But he **always** hears.

**Verse 12 — The Golden Rule:**
8. ***Now Jesus says "SO" — which means because of what he just said. How does the Golden Rule flow out of the Father stuff?*** — Because you've received generosity from your Father, you can afford to be generous with other people. Because you've been forgiven, you can forgive. You treat others the way you want to be treated *because you've been treated that way first.*
9. ***Jesus says "this sums up the Law and the Prophets." That's a huge claim. What does he mean?*** — He's saying: the whole Old Testament boils down to this. Love God. Treat people the way you want to be treated. That's it.

**Say:**
> Jesus is handing us one of the most famous lines ever said — the Golden Rule. *Do to others what you would have them do to you.* You've heard it on posters, bumper stickers, maybe Ms. Whoever's kindergarten wall. But notice where Jesus puts it. Right after he tells us **our Father gives good gifts.**
>
> Because that's where the Golden Rule actually works. When you've been given more than you deserve, you stop trying to ration what you give. When you know you have a generous Father, you can afford to be generous. When you've been loved first, you can love back.
>
> Ask. Seek. Knock. And then overflow into the people around you.

### Ponder the Possibilities

Discuss together:

1. ***What's something you've stopped asking God for because you don't really think he'd answer? Why?***
2. ***When you pray, do you treat God more like a vending machine, a distant boss, or a good Father? Why?***
3. ***Who in your life treats you *better* than you deserve? What does it feel like?***
4. ***The Golden Rule in a middle-school hallway — what would change if you actually did that this week?***
5. ***Jesus says the Golden Rule sums up the whole Old Testament. If it sums up *yours* — how are you doing?***

### Turn Toward Action — Ask and Overflow

Hand each student an index card. Say:
> Two sides tonight.

**Side 1 — What I'm asking for:**
Write down one real thing you're going to start *asking, seeking, and knocking* about this week. Something specific. Then commit: every morning or every night, bring this to your Father.

**Side 2 — How I'm overflowing:**
Write down one specific person this week — and one thing you're going to do for them that you'd want someone to do for you. Sit with them. Stand up for them. Text them. Forgive them. Include them.

Close with prayer. Before the closing prayer, let a few students say — if they want to — what they're asking God for. Pray over those out loud.

End with:
> Father, you love being asked. Your gifts are always good. Teach this group to keep knocking. And because we've received so much from you, help us overflow into every hallway this week. Amen.
