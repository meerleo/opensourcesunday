**Scripture:** Matthew 6:5–15
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

Matthew 6 opens with Jesus warning about "acts of righteousness" performed for show (v.1). He works through three of them — **giving** (vv.2–4), **praying** (vv.5–15), and **fasting** (vv.16–18) — and each time he says the same thing: don't do this for the camera. Do it in the quiet, for an audience of One.

Tonight we camp on prayer. A few things worth holding onto:

- **Verses 5–6** — *Don't pray to be seen.* Jesus isn't against public prayer (he led it himself). He's against prayer as a *performance*. Go into your room, close the door, pray to your Father who sees in secret.
- **Verses 7–8** — *Don't babble.* Jesus names a specific failure: piling on words to manipulate God. He says: *your Father already knows what you need before you ask.* That's actually a comfort, not a criticism. Prayer isn't informing God. It's connecting with him.
- **Verses 9–13 — The Lord's Prayer.** This is the model. Six short petitions. Nothing fancy. It takes 20 seconds to pray.
- **Verses 14–15** — Jesus returns to one line of the prayer and underlines it: *forgive as you have been forgiven.* The forgiveness we offer mirrors the forgiveness we've received. It's not that forgiveness is earned — it's that a forgiven heart *can't stay unforgiving.*

**The Lord's Prayer, phrase by phrase:**

- **"Our Father in heaven"** — Prayer begins with relationship, not religion. "Father" (*Abba*, in Mark's parallel) is how a child addresses a dad. "Our" — not just mine, but ours; the church prays together.
- **"Hallowed be your name"** — May your name be treated as holy. A plea that God's reputation wouldn't be cheapened — starting with my own life.
- **"Your kingdom come, your will be done on earth as it is in heaven"** — Not my will first. Yours. Heaven's rhythms breaking into earth.
- **"Give us today our daily bread"** — Real, practical, today-sized needs. Not a retirement plan — a lunch.
- **"Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors"** — The hinge. We receive mercy in the same measure we extend it (which is why v.14–15 underlines this).
- **"Lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one"** — Protection. Keep us out of places where we know we'll crumble.

**The big idea:** Jesus is de-scaring prayer. Middle schoolers often think prayer is performance, or that they'll say the wrong thing, or that they need fancy words. Jesus says: stop. You already have a Father who already knows. Talk to him like a kid.

{{< callout type="warning" >}}
**A pastoral word on the word "Father."** For some students, "Father" is a loaded word. Their earthly father may be absent, angry, unsafe, or simply unknown. When Jesus teaches us to pray *"Our Father in heaven,"* he is **not** saying *"God is exactly like your dad."* He's saying *"God is the Father you were always meant to have."* If you have a student in the room for whom "Father" lands heavy, don't rush past it — name it out loud. Jesus is offering a Father who is perfectly caring, patient, forgiving, and present. That's the Father every kid in the room deserves, and it's the one they have in God.
{{< /callout >}}

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers
- Optional: a microphone or large prop (used for the "performance prayer" demo)
- Index cards — one per student
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches (for opening)
- Optional: pre-printed cards of the Lord's Prayer for students to take home

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Pray a very short, very simple prayer out loud yourself — something like: *"Jesus, thanks for this group. Help us to hear you tonight. Amen."* This models the whole lesson.

### Catch Their Attention — The Big Show Prayer

**Before the lesson:**
Ask another leader, or a student who can play along, to help. You'll do two prayers back-to-back.

**During the lesson:**

Stand up, grab the microphone prop (or pretend). Say:
> Okay, I'm going to pray. Get ready. This one's gonna be powerful.

Then pray out loud, *ridiculously over the top*:
> *"O most high, magnificent, awesome, incredible God of the universe, who sits enthroned above the heavens, whose majesty is beyond words and whose glory knows no bounds, I come before you humbly, so humbly, so super humbly, to thank you and to praise you and to lift up this assembled gathering on this Wednesday evening…"*

Keep going as long as the students will tolerate it.

Then without commentary, turn to the other leader/student. Say: *"Your turn."*

They pray:
> *"God, thanks for this group. Help us hear you tonight. Amen."*

Look at the students:
> Which one do you think Jesus likes better?

Let them answer. Then say:
> Before we read tonight, I want to tell you this. Jesus has something very specific to say about how to pray — and it's not what most people think.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 6:5–15

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> Jesus is in the middle of the Sermon on the Mount. He's been talking about not doing good stuff for the camera. Now he zeroes in on one specific thing: prayer. He tells us two things NOT to do, and then — because Jesus is a good teacher — he shows us what to do instead.

Read **Matthew 6:5–15** together. Go around the circle.

**Discuss:**

1. ***In verses 5–6, what does Jesus say NOT to do when you pray?*** — Don't pray to be seen. Don't pray to impress. Go into your room, close the door.
2. ***In verses 7–8, what's the second thing Jesus says not to do?*** — Don't babble. Don't pile on words. Your Father already knows what you need.
3. ***Does "your Father already knows what you need" make prayer *more* or *less* important? Why?*** — More. It means prayer isn't us informing God. It's us *connecting* with God. He already knows — he wants to hear from you anyway.
4. ***Now read verses 9–13 again slowly. Notice anything?*** — It's short. Almost weirdly short. Jesus is modeling what he just said.

Then walk the Lord's Prayer **phrase by phrase** on the whiteboard. Write each phrase and stop to unpack it.

- **"Our Father in heaven"** — Who are we talking to? *Father.* Not boss. Not cop. Not distant king. Dad. And "Our" — the whole family praying together.
  - **Pause here if needed.** Acknowledge the room: some of us have great dads, some of us don't. Jesus isn't saying *God is like your dad*. He's saying *God is the Father every one of you was made for* — perfectly patient, perfectly present, perfectly kind. If "Father" is hard for you, stay with it. This is the one who doesn't disappoint.
- **"Hallowed be your name"** — *"May your name be treated as holy."* Starting with my own life. Not dragging God's name through the mud by how I live.
- **"Your kingdom come, your will be done on earth as it is in heaven"** — Whose will comes first? Not mine. His. This is the line that makes prayer *dangerous* — because when you pray it and mean it, it changes what you want.
- **"Give us today our daily bread"** — Real needs. *Today's* needs. Not a five-year plan — a lunch, a ride, the algebra test.
- **"Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors"** — The big one. We ask God to forgive us *in the same way* we forgive others. Jesus comes back to this in verses 14–15 to underline it.
- **"Lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one"** — Protection. Keep me out of the places where I know I fall apart.

Read **verses 14–15** together again.

Ask:
- ***Why does Jesus circle back and underline this one specific line?*** — Because a forgiven heart can't stay unforgiving. If we're walking around receiving God's mercy but refusing to give any, something's not wired right.

**Say:**
> Here's what Jesus just handed us. Prayer isn't performance. You don't score points with God by using big words. You don't impress him with volume. You don't manipulate him with repetition. You walk in as a kid, say what you mean, and trust your Father to hear you — because *he already knows and he already loves you.*
>
> And when he teaches us the model, he keeps it short. Six petitions. Twenty seconds. But every phrase is doing work.

### Ponder the Possibilities

Discuss together:

1. ***What makes prayer feel awkward or hard for you — if it does?***
2. ***Jesus says "WHEN you pray," not "if you pray." Why do you think he assumes his followers are praying?***
3. ***Which phrase of the Lord's Prayer hits different now that we've slowed it down? Why?***
4. ***Be honest: is there someone you're asking God to forgive you, but you haven't forgiven? What do you think Jesus would say to you about that?***
5. ***What's one thing you *don't* pray about right now — that you probably could?***

### Turn Toward Action — Pray It for a Week

Hand each student an index card (or a pre-printed Lord's Prayer card if you have them). Say:
> Here's your challenge. Pray the Lord's Prayer every day this week. In bed. At breakfast. On the bus. In class before a test. Doesn't matter where. Just pray it.
>
> Don't rush it. Don't mumble through it. Actually mean each phrase. See what happens after a week.

Pray the Lord's Prayer **together** as a whole group to close the lesson:

> Our Father in heaven,
> hallowed be your name,
> your kingdom come,
> your will be done,
> on earth as it is in heaven.
> Give us today our daily bread.
> And forgive us our debts,
> as we also have forgiven our debtors.
> And lead us not into temptation,
> but deliver us from the evil one.
> Amen.
