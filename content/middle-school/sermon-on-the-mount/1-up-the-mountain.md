**Scripture:** Matthew 5:1–12
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

By the end of Matthew 4, Jesus has become the most talked-about rabbi in the region. He's been traveling all over Galilee, teaching in synagogues, proclaiming the good news of the kingdom, and healing every kind of sickness and pain. Crowds from Galilee, the Decapolis, Jerusalem, Judea, and beyond the Jordan are chasing him down. Then, in Matthew 5:1, he sees the crowd, walks up a mountainside, sits down (the posture of a teacher in that day), and begins the most famous sermon ever preached.

Before Jesus says a single word about how to live, he tells us *who God is cheering for*. The "Beatitudes" (from the Latin *beatus* — blessed, happy, flourishing) are not a checklist of behaviors to unlock God's favor. They are Jesus announcing that God's kingdom runs on an **upside-down scoreboard**. The people the world calls losers — the poor in spirit, the mourners, the meek, the hungry, the merciful, the pure in heart, the peacemakers, the persecuted — God calls blessed. That would have sounded as scandalous then as it does now.

For middle schoolers, this is enormous. They live in a world where "blessed" almost always means *rich, pretty, followed, talented, winning*. Jesus just took a sharpie to that whole list.

**Two patterns worth pointing out:**

- **Every Beatitude has the same shape:** *Blessed are \_\_\_, for they will \_\_\_.* A condition, then a promise.
- **The bookends match:** verses 3 and 10 both end *"for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."* Jesus wraps the whole list in the same promise — this isn't a waiting game. The kingdom is already here for these people.

This lesson is the first of two in the Sermon on the Mount series. Next week we fly through the rest of the sermon (Matt 5:13–7:27). Tonight we stay parked on the Beatitudes.

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers (two colors)
- Index cards — one per student
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches (for opening)
- Optional: a printed card for each student with all 8 Beatitudes to take home

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Invite a student to lead in prayer. Thank God for the gift of the group, and ask the Holy Spirit to open hearts to the words of Jesus.

### Catch Their Attention — The Scoreboard

**Before the lesson:**
Write across the top of the whiteboard in big letters: **"WHO IS BLESSED?"**

**During the lesson:**
Say:
> Raise your hand if you've ever heard someone say *"I'm blessed"* — on a post, in a song, on a jersey, anywhere. When people say that, what do they usually mean?

Let students shout out answers. Write them on the board in one color. (You'll probably get: rich, healthy, popular, good family, winning, famous, lots of followers, talented, pretty, etc.)

Then say:
> Okay — same question, but now in *Jesus'* version. If Jesus wrote the list, who do you think he'd put on it?

Don't give the answer yet. Let them guess. Write their guesses in a **different color.** Then say:
> Hold onto that. Let's read what Jesus actually says — and see how close we got.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 5:1–12

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> Over the next two weeks we're going to be in the most famous sermon Jesus ever preached — something called **the Sermon on the Mount.** Three whole chapters of Jesus teaching us how to live in God's kingdom. Today we're reading the very beginning — who showed up, where Jesus sat down, and the first words out of his mouth: eight blessings we call *the Beatitudes*.

Read **Matthew 5:1–12** together. Go around the circle; each student reads one verse. Anyone who doesn't want to read can say *"pass"* — no pressure.

**Discuss:**

1. ***Before Jesus even says a word, what kind of crowd is showing up?*** *(look back at Matt 4:23–25)* — Sick people. Hurting people. People from everywhere. Not the cool kids.
2. ***Jesus uses the word "blessed" over and over. How is his idea of "blessed" different from the one on our board?***
3. ***Do you notice a pattern in how each Beatitude is worded?*** — *Blessed are \_\_\_, for they will \_\_\_.* A condition, then a promise.
4. ***Look at verses 3 and 10. What do they have in common?*** — Same promise: theirs **is** the kingdom of heaven. Jesus bookends the whole list with it. The kingdom isn't a someday thing — it's here, already, for these people.

Then walk through the eight blessings. Don't lecture each one — ask, let them answer, fill in where needed.

- **"Poor in spirit" (v.3)** — Spiritually empty-handed. Knowing you don't have it all together. The opposite of proud.
- **"Those who mourn" (v.4)** — Why would Jesus bless grieving people? Because God is the God who *comforts*.
- **"The meek" (v.5)** — Meek isn't weak. Meek is strength under control. Think a trained warhorse. Think Moses. Think Jesus.
- **"Hunger and thirst for righteousness" (v.6)** — A gut-level *craving* for what's right. And the promise: *they will be filled.*
- **"The merciful" (v.7)** — Mercy isn't just being nice. Mercy is giving someone what they don't deserve — because God gave us what we didn't deserve.
- **"Pure in heart" (v.8)** — Not "perfect." *Undivided.* Same person online, at school, at home, at Crossroads.
- **"Peacemakers" (v.9)** — Not peace-*keepers* (who stay quiet to avoid a fight). Peace-*makers* step into conflict to heal it. That's what God did for us.
- **"Persecuted for righteousness" (vv.10–12)** — Following Jesus sometimes costs something. Jesus says: *rejoice* — you're in good company with the prophets.

**Say:**
> Here's what Jesus is doing. He is flipping the world's scoreboard upside down. The world cheers for people who have it all together. Jesus cheers for people who know they don't. The world runs *away* from people who are grieving. Jesus runs *toward* them. The world worships the loudest person in the room. Jesus blesses the one who doesn't need to be.
>
> These eight blessings are going to change how we look at our school, our team, our lunch table — and our own hearts.

### Ponder the Possibilities

Discuss together:

1. ***Looking back at our board — how different is Jesus' list from what the world calls "blessed"?***
2. ***Which of the eight Beatitudes hits you the hardest right now? Not the easiest. The one you need the most.***
3. ***Who in your school do most people ignore or laugh at — who Jesus might actually be calling "blessed"?***
4. ***Have you ever felt like one of the people on Jesus' list? (Poor in spirit, grieving, small, overlooked?) What would it mean to you to hear Jesus call you "blessed" right there?***
5. ***Why do you think Jesus started his biggest sermon with this list? What is he trying to do to us before he teaches us anything else?***

### Turn Toward Action — Name One

Hand each student an index card. Say:
> On this card, write the name of one person in your life who fits Jesus' list more than the world's list. You don't have to show anyone. Just write the name.
>
> Slip it in your pocket, your phone case, or your Bible. Your job this week: pray for that person every day. And do *one* thing — literally one thing — that tells them they matter. Say hi. Save them a seat. Text them. Invite them. Anything.

End with prayer:

> Jesus, thank you that your blessing doesn't depend on the scoreboard of the world. That your blessing is given to the meek, the mourning, the peacemakers, and the pour in spirit; because that is who we are. Give us your eyes this week for the people on our cards. Amen.
