**Scripture:** Matthew 5:9–12
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

We've watched Jesus flip the scoreboard (Lesson 1). We've watched him zoom in on the inside — hunger, mercy, pure heart (Lesson 2). Now he turns the camera outward. The last Beatitudes are about what the blessed life looks like when it **costs you something.**

- **"Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God." (v.9)** — There's a huge difference between peace-*keepers* (people who avoid trouble by staying quiet) and peace-*makers* (people who walk *into* conflict to heal it). Jesus himself is the ultimate peacemaker — he didn't keep his distance from our mess; he stepped right into it. Being called a "child of God" in this verse isn't a generic title; it's saying *you look like your Father.* Peacemakers carry the family resemblance.
- **"Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." (v.10)** — This one stings. Jesus isn't blessing people who are suffering because they're being jerks. He's blessing people who are paying a price for *doing the right thing*. Notice the payoff: same as the very first Beatitude — *the kingdom of heaven.* The series comes full circle.
- **"Blessed are you when people insult you, persecute you, and falsely say all kinds of evil against you because of me. Rejoice and be glad, because great is your reward in heaven, for in the same way they persecuted the prophets who were before you." (vv.11–12)** — Jesus gets personal here. He shifts from "blessed are *they*" to "blessed are *you*." And the response is not *endure it* or *survive it* — it's *rejoice and be glad.* You're in good company with every prophet who ever spoke up for God.

Middle schoolers know what it costs to stand up. They know what it's like to be made fun of, excluded, or called names for being "too churchy," "too nice," "too different." Jesus isn't minimizing that pain. He's telling them: *I see it. I've been there. And you are not alone — you are blessed.*

{{< callout type="info" >}}
*What are we willing to stand for when it costs us something? And are we peacemakers — or just peacekeepers?*
{{< /callout >}}

*What are the most important takeaways?*

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers
- Scenario cards (prepped ahead — see below)
- A small bandage or band-aid for each student (for Turn toward Action)
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches
- Optional: a worship track ready to play softly at the end (something like "So Will I," "Graves Into Gardens," or "Stand in Your Love")

### Scenario Cards

Prep before class — write each on an index card:

1. Your friend group is roasting a kid who isn't there. Everyone's laughing. They look at you.
2. You see a new kid eating lunch alone. Your table is already full of your best friends.
3. Two of your friends are fighting and won't talk to each other. Both are venting to *you*.
4. Someone at school finds out you go to church and says, *"So you actually believe that stuff?"*
5. A post goes around with a rumor about someone you barely know. Everyone's sharing it.
6. Your teammate is cheating off your test. They'd be really mad if you said anything.

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Invite a student to pray, or pray yourself — asking God for courage tonight.

### Catch Their Attention — Peacekeeper or Peacemaker?

**Before the lesson:**
On the whiteboard, draw two columns: **PEACE-KEEPER** | **PEACE-MAKER.** Leave them blank.

**During the lesson:**
Say:
> There are two kinds of people in the world when things get uncomfortable. Peace-*keepers* and peace-*makers*. Let's figure out the difference.

Ask the group to help you fill in each column. Prompt if they get stuck:

- *A peace-keeper does what to avoid a fight?* (Stays quiet. Walks away. Pretends it's fine. Agrees with whoever is loudest.)
- *A peace-maker does what?* (Steps in. Tells the truth in love. Apologizes first. Asks hard questions. Makes things right, not just quiet.)

Then say:
> Here's the catch: peace-keeping is **cheaper**. Peace-making **costs something** — your reputation, your comfort, sometimes your friend group. Tonight Jesus is going to tell us which one he calls blessed. Spoiler: it's not the cheap one.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 5:9–12

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> For two weeks we've been in Jesus' Sermon on the Mount. We've heard him bless the poor in spirit, the mourners, the meek, the hungry, the merciful, and the pure in heart. Tonight we read the last Beatitudes — and they're about what happens when living the blessed life actually *costs* you something.

Read **Matthew 5:9–12** together, going around the circle.

**Discuss:**

1. ***"Peacemaker" vs. "peacekeeper" — which one is Jesus blessing? Why does it matter?***
2. ***Jesus says peacemakers will be called "children of God." What do you think that means?*** — They look like their Father. God is a peacemaker — he stepped into our mess through Jesus. When we step into conflict to heal it, we carry the family resemblance.
3. ***"Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness." What does *persecuted* mean? Can you be persecuted in middle school?*** — Yes. Being made fun of, excluded, talked about, eye-rolled at — for doing what's right. It's real.
4. ***Jesus says "rejoice and be glad." How is that even possible when you're being made fun of?*** — Because you're in good company. You're standing with every prophet who ever spoke up, and you're standing with Jesus himself. You're not crazy. You're not alone.
5. ***In verse 11, Jesus switches from "blessed are *they*" to "blessed are *you*." Why do you think that matters?*** — He's getting personal. He's saying: *I see you. I know what this costs. I'm calling YOU blessed.*

**Say:**
> Here's what Jesus is doing. He started this sermon by flipping the scoreboard. Now he's closing the Beatitudes by telling us: when you actually *live* this way — when you're hungry for the right things, showing mercy, being the same person everywhere, making peace, standing up for truth — it will cost you sometimes.
>
> And when it does, don't be shocked. Don't be ashamed. **Rejoice.** You're in the family. You look like Jesus. You're standing where the prophets stood. Great is your reward in heaven.

### Ponder the Possibilities — Scenario Round

Hand out the scenario cards (one per student or per pair). Say:
> Read your card out loud. Then tell us: *what would the peace-keeper do? What would the peace-maker do? What would it cost to be the peace-maker?*

Walk through each one together. Don't rush. Let the Shepherds in the room weigh in. Some of these will get real.

Extra discussion:

1. ***Can you think of a time you were a peacekeeper when you wish you'd been a peacemaker?***
2. ***What does it cost to stand up for someone at your school? What does it cost to stay silent?***
3. ***Is there someone in your life right now who you know God is nudging you to stand up for — or to make peace with?***

### Turn Toward Action — The Band-Aid

Give every student a bandage. Say:
> Band-aids don't prevent wounds. They cover them while they heal. Peacemakers are kind of like that — they don't stop the world from being broken; they step into the broken places and help heal them.
>
> Take this band-aid home. Stick it somewhere you'll see it this week — your water bottle, your binder, your mirror. Every time you see it, ask yourself one question: *"Who am I being called to make peace with or stand up for — right now?"*

End with prayer. Play a worship track softly as you pray:

> Jesus, thank you that you didn't keep your distance from our mess. You stepped right into it. Make us peacemakers this week. Give us the guts to stand up when it costs us something. And remind us — when it's hard — that we're in *your* family, and we are blessed. Amen.
