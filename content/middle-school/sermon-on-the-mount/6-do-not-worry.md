**Scripture:** Matthew 6:25–34
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

Middle school is a worry factory. Grades. Friend drama. Body stuff. Family stuff. Tests. The lunch table. That group chat. The future. What people think. What *God* thinks. The pile never stops growing.

Jesus knows this. In Matthew 6:25–34, he walks right into the middle of it and says one of the most direct, pastoral things in the whole Sermon on the Mount: **"Therefore I tell you, do not worry."**

A few things to know before you teach:

- **The "therefore" matters.** This passage flows directly out of Matt 6:19–24, where Jesus says *you cannot serve both God and money.* So when he says "therefore do not worry," he's not handing out an inspirational quote. He's saying: *if your treasure is in heaven and your master is God, worry doesn't fit anymore.*
- **Jesus gives two illustrations — both from nature.**
  - **The birds (v.26):** They don't plant, harvest, or store anything. God feeds them anyway. *"Are you not much more valuable than they?"*
  - **The lilies (vv.28–30):** They don't work or sew clothes, and yet Solomon at his richest was never dressed like one. *"If that is how God clothes the grass of the field, will he not much more clothe you?"*
- **Worry is useless.** Verse 27: *"Can any one of you by worrying add a single hour to your life?"* Jesus isn't dismissing what students feel — he's pointing out that worry doesn't actually *do* anything.
- **The pivot is verse 33.** Don't just *not worry* — instead, **seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well.** Worry is replaced by a different orientation entirely. Trust, and a different priority.
- **Verse 34 is the middle-school mic drop.** *"Therefore do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow will worry about itself. Each day has enough trouble of its own."*

Middle schoolers don't need to be scolded for worrying. They need to hear that their Father knows what they need, has been feeding sparrows and clothing grass for a very long time, and is still paying attention to them.

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- A jar, shoebox, or empty tissue box (the "worry jar")
- Small slips of paper or sticky notes (one per student, plus extras)
- A small flower, plant, or even a photo of a field in bloom
- A photo or video of birds (YouTube clip works, but keep it short — under a minute)
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers
- Index cards — one per student
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches (for opening)

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Invite a student to lead in prayer. Tonight is about a pile of real worries — ask the Holy Spirit to come close to every anxious middle schooler in the room.

### Catch Their Attention — The Worry Jar

**Before the lesson:**
Set the jar or box on the table with a stack of slips and pens nearby. Place the flower/plant and a printed bird picture beside it — but don't call attention to them yet.

**During the lesson:**
Hand every student a slip of paper. Say:
> You've got sixty seconds. No names. No sharing. Write down the top two or three things weighing on you right now. The stuff that pops into your head when you can't fall asleep. The worries that hang out in the back of your mind during class.

Give them a full minute. Then ask them to fold the slips and drop them in the jar without looking at anyone else's. Don't read them aloud.

Say:
> Every one of those worries is riding on your shoulders right now. Tonight Jesus has something to say about that pile — and it's not what you might think.

Gesture at the flower and the bird picture.
> Keep an eye on these two. They're gonna come back.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 6:25–34

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> Quick recap: Jesus has been preaching his famous Sermon on the Mount. Right before tonight's passage, he told everyone you can't serve both God *and* money — you have to pick. Then he drops this word: "**therefore.**" That word matters. It means what he's about to say is built on what he just said.

Read **Matthew 6:25–34** together, going around the circle.

**Discuss:**

1. ***What's the very first command Jesus gives here in v.25?*** — *Do not worry.*
2. ***What two illustrations does he use? Why those?*** — **Birds** and **flowers** (lilies). Both are cared for by God without trying. Jesus is saying: if God takes care of sparrows and grass, he's obviously paying attention to *you*.
3. ***Verse 27 — "Can any one of you by worrying add a single hour to your life?" What's Jesus getting at?*** — Worry doesn't actually *fix* anything. It *feels* productive. It isn't.
4. ***In v.32 Jesus says the "pagans" run after food, drink, and clothes. What's he saying about what makes Christians different?*** — Not what we have — *what we trust*. The pagan strategy is to chase these things and panic. The Christian strategy is to trust the Father who already knows what we need.
5. ***Verse 33 — what does Jesus say to do *instead* of worrying?*** — *Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness.*
6. ***What does that actually mean for a middle schooler?*** — Make God's priorities your first priorities. Live the way he's teaching you to live. Trust him to handle the stuff you can't.
7. ***Verse 34 — "Don't worry about tomorrow." Why do you think Jesus adds that?*** — Because most worry is about tomorrow. It hasn't even happened yet. Today has enough going on. Stay here.

**Say:**
> Worry feels like we're caring — like if we just worry hard enough, something gets fixed. Jesus says: nope. Worry is a treadmill. It burns a ton of energy and gets you nowhere.
>
> Here's his logic: your Father in heaven feeds every bird in the sky and colors every wildflower in the field. He has been doing that for longer than anyone reading this has been alive. Are you really gonna tell me he forgot about you?
>
> And then Jesus pivots. He doesn't just say *stop worrying.* He gives us something to do instead: **seek first his kingdom.** Trust him with the stuff you can't control. Put his priorities first. Live today like he's actually your Father. And tomorrow? Tomorrow will worry about itself.

### Ponder the Possibilities

Discuss together:

1. ***What's the biggest thing you worry about right now? (You don't have to say out loud — but be honest with yourself.)***
2. ***Has worrying about that thing ever actually fixed it? Or does it just keep it on repeat?***
3. ***What would "seeking first the kingdom" look like for a middle schooler this week? Not abstractly — concretely.***
4. ***Be honest: do you actually believe God knows what you need? Or does it feel like he forgot about you?***
5. ***Jesus ends with "tomorrow will worry about itself." What's one worry about tomorrow you could set down tonight?***

### Turn Toward Action — Hand It Over

Pick up the worry jar. Don't read the slips. Just hold it up.

Say:
> These are yours. Every one of these worries is real. Jesus isn't saying they're silly. He's saying — **hand them over to a Father who's been feeding the sparrows for a long time.**

Hand each student a fresh index card. Have them write:

1. **ONE worry I'm handing to God this week.** (Be specific.)
2. **ONE kingdom-first thing I'll do** instead of spinning on that worry. (Pray about it and let it go when the thought comes back. Text a friend instead of doom-scrolling. Read a Psalm before bed. Show up to Crossroads when I'd rather stay home.)

Close together. Pray over the jar. Thank God that he already knows. Ask him to take the pile. Close with Philippians 4:6–7 if you want to read one more scripture: *"Do not be anxious about anything… and the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."*
