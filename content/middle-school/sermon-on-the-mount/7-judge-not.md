**Scripture:** Matthew 7:1–6
**Series:** [Sermon on the Mount](../)

## Behind the Curtain
*What do I need to know before teaching?*

Matthew 7:1 is *the* most often-quoted verse by people who don't actually go to church. *"Do not judge, or you too will be judged."* It gets used to mean *"don't ever say anything is right or wrong"* — which is almost the opposite of what Jesus is saying.

Here's what Jesus actually packs into these six verses:

- **Verse 1–2:** *"Do not judge, or you too will be judged. For in the same way you judge others, you will be judged, and with the measure you use, it will be measured to you."* Jesus isn't banning moral discernment — he literally just told us (earlier in the sermon) to watch for false prophets and guard our hearts. What he's banning is **the posture of the judge.** The smug, stand-above, contempt kind of judging. He's warning: *whatever yardstick you use on people will come back around on you.*
- **Verses 3–5 — The Speck and the Plank.** This is the comedic-on-purpose centerpiece. Jesus paints a ridiculous picture: you have a *two-by-four sticking out of your face* and you're leaning in to fix a speck of sawdust in someone else's eye. It's absurd. It's meant to be. Jesus is not saying "never help your brother or sister" — he says **"first take the plank out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother's eye."** Self-examination **before** correction, not instead of it.
- **Verse 6** — *"Do not give dogs what is sacred; do not throw your pearls to pigs."* This feels like a sudden pivot, but Jesus is balancing his point. He's not saying "accept everything, call nothing wrong." He's saying: *use discernment about what's precious.* Some people aren't ready to hear truth, and forcing it can do harm. Judgment is out; discernment is in.

**The core idea for middle schoolers:** you can care enough about someone to help them see clearly — but first you have to deal with what you're not seeing about yourself. The move isn't *don't judge anything.* The move is *don't judge from a throne you don't belong on.*

Middle school is a ruthless ranking machine. Judgment is the native language. Jesus is inviting a different posture — humble, self-aware, merciful.

## Prop Department
*What supplies do I need?*

- Bibles, one per student
- **A 2×4 piece of wood** (or even a yardstick — something obviously big) and **a tiny speck of something** (a single grain of sawdust, a crumb, a pencil shaving)
- Whiteboard or large paper + markers
- Index cards — one per student
- Pens/pencils
- Christ light / candle + matches (for opening)

## Directing the Scene
*What learning activities might we do?*

### Start with Prayer

Light the Christ light. Invite a student to lead in prayer. Tonight's lesson cuts — ask the Holy Spirit to make us less interested in other people's specks and more honest about our own planks.

### Catch Their Attention — Plank in Your Eye

**Before the lesson:**
Have the 2×4 (or yardstick) lying in plain sight. Place a tiny crumb or grain of something on a napkin nearby.

**During the lesson:**
Hold up the tiny speck dramatically.
> Okay. Everyone look really hard at this. What is it?

Let them try to identify it. Then pick up the 2×4.
> Now. Imagine I have this sticking out of my face. Like, right in my eye. Right now. Big 2×4, just… bam, in the eye. And instead of dealing with that, I'm over here trying to pick that tiny speck out of your eyelashes. How do you think that's gonna go?

Let them laugh. Make it goofy.

> Hold onto that picture. Jesus actually tells this joke tonight. And he means it.

### Read and Interpret — Matthew 7:1–6

**Intro for the youth:** Before reading, say:
> Matthew 7:1 is the most misused verse of the entire Bible. You've probably heard someone quote it to mean *"don't ever say anything is right or wrong."* That's not what Jesus is saying. Listen carefully — Jesus is going after a specific kind of judging, and he's hilarious about it.

Read **Matthew 7:1–6** together.

**Discuss:**

**Verses 1–2:**
1. ***What does Jesus warn is going to happen to us if we judge others harshly?*** — The same measure gets used on us. The yardstick you hold up on others will come back and land on you.
2. ***What does that tell us about what *kind* of judging Jesus is against?*** — The smug, above-it-all kind. Not the kind that says "that's not right." The kind that says "…and I would never."

**Verses 3–5:**
3. ***Draw the picture Jesus is painting.*** — A person with a 2×4 sticking out of their face, leaning in to help someone with a tiny piece of sawdust in their eye.
4. ***Why do you think Jesus makes this sound so ridiculous?*** — Because it IS. We do it all the time, and it's absurd. We'll rip on someone for a flaw we're twice as guilty of.
5. ***Jesus doesn't say "never help your brother." What does he say?*** — **First** take the plank out of your own eye. **Then** you'll see clearly to help.

**Verse 6:**
6. ***This one sounds weird after the speck-and-plank stuff. What does it balance?*** — Jesus isn't saying "never discern anything." He's saying: use wisdom about what's precious. Don't force truth on people who aren't ready. Judgment is out; discernment is in.

**Say:**
> Here's what Jesus is doing. He is not saying, *"never call anything wrong."* He's saying, *"drop the gavel."* The seat of judgment is not for you. It's not for me. It's for God.
>
> That doesn't mean we don't care about what's right. It doesn't mean we let our friends crash. It just means — *start with yourself.* When you feel the urge to go after someone else, first ask: *what's the plank I'm carrying? What am I doing that's way bigger than what I'm upset about?*
>
> Because here's the wild thing Jesus promises. Once you pull *your* plank? You'll actually see clearly enough to help. Judgment makes you blind. Humility makes you a good friend.

### Ponder the Possibilities

Discuss together:

1. ***Have you ever had somebody call you out for something when they were clearly doing the same thing ten times worse? How did that feel?***
2. ***What's a "plank" you've been carrying that's way bigger than the "specks" you're always noticing in other people?***
3. ***Middle school is built on judgment — roasts, rankings, the unspoken lunch-table pecking order. What would it look like if you refused to play?***
4. ***Is there someone you've been quietly judging this week whose story you don't actually know?***
5. ***Jesus says "the measure you use will be measured to you." If someone used YOUR yardstick on YOU this week, how would it go?***

### Turn Toward Action — Plank Check

Hand each student an index card. Say:
> Two sides of the card. One side is honest. One side is the move.

**Side 1:**
1. **One speck** I've been harping on in someone else's life.
2. **One plank** I've been ignoring in my own.

**Side 2:**
3. **One thing I'm going to do this week to pull my plank** — apologize, confess, get help, talk to someone, pray about it honestly.

If you've been harping on someone else, the card is also a little reminder: *pull your plank first.*

Close with prayer. Ask God for eyes that see our own stuff clearly and eyes that are slower to judge everyone else.
