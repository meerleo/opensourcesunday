      Lesson 2 · Blessed Are the Hungry

      Scripture: Matthew 5:6–8 Series: Sermon on the Mount

      Behind the Curtain

      What do I need to know before teaching?

      Last week we set the stage: Jesus went up the mountain, sat down, and started flipping the world’s scoreboard upside down. He blessed the poor in spirit, those who mourn, and the meek. This week we move into the middle three Beatitudes — and they each zoom in on something inside us.

      • “Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they will be filled.” (v.6) — This is not a polite wish to be a better person. This is a gut-level craving for things to be right — right with God, right with ourselves, right in the world. Jesus is blessing the people who are starving for the things God cares about.
      • “Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy.” (v.7) — Mercy isn’t just being nice. Mercy is giving someone what they don’t deserve because God gave us what we didn’t deserve. The merciful don’t keep score. They let go of the right to get even.
      • “Blessed are the pure in heart, for they will see God.” (v.8) — “Pure in heart” isn’t about never messing up. In the Old Testament, purity meant undivided — like pure gold, unmixed. Jesus is blessing people whose inside matches their outside. Same person on Instagram, in the hallway, at the dinner table, at Crossroads.

      Middle schoolers live in a world that tells them the outside is what matters — the fit, the follower count, the highlight reel. Jesus slides in and says the thing God is actually paying attention to is the inside — what you crave, how you treat people who can’t pay you back, and whether you’re the same person everywhere.

      Are we hungry for the right things? Do we give mercy as freely as we want to receive it? Are we the same person when no one’s watching as when everyone is?

      Center Stage

      What are the most important takeaways?

      God cares about what’s underneath. The Beatitudes bless the people whose cravings, mercy, and integrity are shaped by him — not by the highlight reel.

      Prop Department

      What supplies do I need?

      • Bibles, one per student
      • A tray of snacks students actually want (pretzels, cookies, chips — something with smell), covered with a cloth napkin or foil
      • Sticky notes or small slips of paper
      • Pens/pencils
      • Whiteboard or large paper + markers
      • Christ light / candle + matches
      • Three signs/printouts (one per wall): HUNGER · MERCY · PURE HEART

      Directing the Scene

      What learning activities might we do?

      Start with Prayer

      Light the Christ light. Popcorn prayer: invite each student to name one thing they’re thankful for in a single word.

      Catch Their Attention — The Snack That’s Not Yet

      Before the lesson: Set the covered tray of snacks on the middle of the table or floor, in plain sight, so students can smell it as they come in. Do not uncover it yet.

      During the lesson: As students notice and start asking about the snacks, smile and say:

      Yeah. They’re real. They’re here. But we’re not eating them yet.

      Let the suspense build for a minute. Then ask:

      What’s your favorite food you’d wait in line forever for? Stand in line for an hour. Drive for it. Beg your parents for it. What is it?

      Let every student answer. Then ask:

      Now, what if I told you Jesus once said the most blessed people in God’s kingdom are the ones who crave the right things as badly as you crave that food?

      Say:

      Tonight we’re reading three verses about what’s underneath — what we want, how we treat people, and who we really are. Then we’ll eat. Pinky promise.

      Read and Interpret — Matthew 5:6–8

      Intro for the youth: Before reading, say:

      Last week we started Jesus’ famous Sermon on the Mount. We heard him call the poor, the grieving, and the meek blessed. Tonight we’re reading the next three lines — and every one of them zooms in on what’s going on inside us. Let’s read together.

      Read Matthew 5:6–8 together. Go around the circle; each student reads one verse. Anyone who doesn’t want to read can say “pass.”

      Discuss:

      1. Jesus uses hunger and thirst as a picture. Why those two? What do hunger and thirst feel like? — Urgent. Can’t ignore it. It takes over.
      2. “Hunger and thirst for righteousness” — what do you think that looks like in real life? — Actually wanting what God wants. Caring when something is unfair. Caring about your friend being hurt. Caring about your own heart.
      3. “Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy.” What’s the difference between being nice and being merciful? — Nice is polite. Mercy is giving someone what they don’t deserve — forgiveness, a second chance, a seat at the table — because God gave it to you first.
      4. “Pure in heart” — does that mean “perfect”? — No. It means undivided. Same person everywhere. No fake version online, no different version at home, no different version here. Just you.
      5. Which of these three is hardest for you — hungering for the right things, showing mercy, or being the same person in every room?

      Say:

      Here’s the wild thing about these three. The world teaches us to curate the outside — the post, the fit, the bio, the team, the grade. Jesus walks in and asks: what’s underneath? What do you actually crave? How do you actually treat people who can’t do anything for you? Who are you when the camera’s off?

      And here’s the promise in each one: those who hunger will be filled. The merciful will receive mercy. The pure in heart will see God. Jesus isn’t saying: get it right and then you’ll be blessed. He’s saying: if God is already changing what’s underneath, you’re already in the blessed life.

      Ponder the Possibilities

      Set up the three signs — HUNGER · MERCY · PURE HEART — on three different walls.

      Discuss together:

      1. What do middle schoolers hunger for the most? What are your friends craving? What are you craving?
      2. Who has shown you mercy that you didn’t deserve? What did it feel like? Who in your life needs mercy from you right now?
      3. Where in your life is it hardest to be the same person? Online vs. in person? At school vs. at home? With one friend vs. another? Why?
      4. Jesus says the pure in heart will “see God.” What do you think that actually means? — God shows up in places where we’re not hiding. The undivided heart gets to see him.

      Turn Toward Action — Pick Your Wall

      Hand out sticky notes. Say:

      Here’s your move. Look at the three walls: Hunger. Mercy. Pure Heart. Pick the one where God is tapping you on the shoulder this week. Write on your sticky note one concrete thing you’re going to do — and stick it on that wall.

      Examples: “Hunger” — I’ll read one chapter of Matthew this week. “Mercy” — I’ll forgive [name] and stop holding it over them. “Pure Heart” — I’ll delete/hide that thing I’m hiding. I’ll be the same person at school as I am here.

      Give them time to write. Let them stick their notes up. Take a picture of each wall (Photographer role).

      Uncover the snacks. Say:

      I told you we’d eat. Here’s the thing — Jesus said the hungry will be filled. God doesn’t start this craving in us and then leave us starving. He promises to show up. So eat, and remember: the same God who filled you tonight is filling what’s underneath when you ask him to.

      End with prayer. Thank God that he cares about what’s inside — and that he’s the one who fills it.
